# How Does On-Policy Distillation Change a Model’s Internal Computation?

## A Controlled Mechanistic Study of Planning-Site Formation and Capability Retention in Rhyming-Couplet Generation

## Abstract

On-policy distillation trains a student model on prefixes sampled from the student’s own policy, with a stronger teacher supplying token-level supervision at those student-visited states. This differs from conventional off-policy distillation, where the student performs supervised fine-tuning on fixed teacher-generated trajectories. It also differs from ordinary supervised fine-tuning on a fixed external dataset, where the target responses were not generated by either the teacher or the student.

Recent behavioral evidence suggests that training on on-policy data can produce less catastrophic forgetting than static supervised fine-tuning. However, it remains unclear what mechanistically differs inside the model under these training regimes. Does on-policy training preserve the student’s existing computational pathways? Does off-policy imitation relocate information into new token positions or circuits? Are apparent benefits of on-policy distillation caused by student-generated prefixes, dense teacher probabilities, or simply higher-quality training data?

This project will compare four training regimes from the same student initialization:

1. supervised fine-tuning on a fixed corpus of existing rhyming poems;
2. off-policy distillation through supervised fine-tuning on fixed teacher-generated traces;
3. off-policy soft knowledge distillation on those same teacher-generated prefixes;
4. on-policy soft knowledge distillation on student-generated prefixes.

Rhyming-couplet generation provides a clean latent-planning task: the model must preserve a phonological constraint established by the final word of the first line while generating the second line. Prior work found that future-rhyme information is often linearly decodable at the newline token, but only Gemma-3-27B causally relies on this representation. In that model, the causal locus of the rhyme constraint migrates from the original rhyme word to the newline around layer 30, with most of the routing effect concentrated in five attention heads. 

We will train a smaller Gemma student under each regime and use longitudinal linear probing, activation patching, path patching, representation-similarity analysis, and general-capability evaluations to determine whether the methods learn the same behavior through different internal computations. The central hypothesis is that on-policy distillation will more often adapt the student’s existing causal computation, while static off-policy training will cause broader representational reorganization and greater interference with unrelated capabilities.

## 1. Motivation

Autoregressive language models are trained to predict the next token given a prefix. The distribution of those prefixes therefore matters.

In ordinary supervised fine-tuning, the model trains on prefixes taken from a fixed dataset:

[
s_t=(x,y_{<t}), \qquad (x,y)\sim D_{\mathrm{fixed}}.
]

In off-policy teacher-trace distillation, a stronger teacher first generates a response:

[
y\sim\pi_T(\cdot\mid x),
]

and the student is then trained by teacher-forced cross-entropy on prefixes from that teacher trajectory:

[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{teacher\text{-}trace}}
===========================================

-\mathbb{E}*{x,,y\sim\pi_T}
\sum_t
\log \pi*\theta(y_t\mid x,y_{<t}).
]

During deployment, however, the student conditions on its own previous outputs. Once it deviates from the fixed training trajectory, it may enter states that were absent from training.

On-policy distillation instead samples trajectories from the current student:

[
y\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid x),
]

and asks the teacher to provide supervision at the student’s actual prefixes:

[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OPD}}
==========================

\mathbb{E}*{s\sim d*{\pi_\theta}}
D\left(
\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s),
\pi_T(\cdot\mid s)
\right).
]

The central difference is therefore the state distribution:

[
\text{Fixed-corpus SFT: }s\sim D_{\mathrm{external}}
]

[
\text{Teacher-trace distillation: }s\sim d_{\pi_T}
]

[
\text{On-policy distillation: }s\sim d_{\pi_\theta}.
]

Behavioral work suggests that on-policy training may reduce forgetting because gradients are applied near states the student already visits. The student may be able to improve by modifying its existing behavior locally rather than learning to reproduce a foreign trajectory distribution.

This explanation remains incomplete. It does not reveal whether on-policy and off-policy training:

* modify different layers;
* preserve or replace existing circuits;
* encode future constraints at different token positions;
* create different attention pathways;
* or produce different levels of representational interference on unrelated inputs.

This proposal studies those questions directly.

## 2. Primary Research Question

**When fixed-corpus SFT, teacher-trace SFT, and on-policy distillation produce comparable improvements in rhyming-couplet generation, do they implement that behavior through different internal computations?**

### Secondary questions

1. Does off-policy teacher-trace imitation cause the student to acquire the teacher’s planning-site organization?

2. Does ordinary fixed-corpus SFT produce the same mechanistic changes as teacher-trace SFT, or are those changes specific to teacher-generated trajectories?

3. Does on-policy distillation preserve the student’s original causal pathway more effectively?

4. Does one training regime create a causally active representation at the newline while another continues to access the original rhyme word?

5. Are mechanistic differences caused by the source of the prefixes or by hard versus soft teacher supervision?

6. Are reduced representational drift and reduced capability forgetting associated with reuse of the student’s original computation?

7. Does reverse-KL on-policy distillation improve rhyme accuracy by collapsing onto a narrow set of common poetic continuations?

## 3. Core Hypotheses

### H1: On-policy training causes less general representational drift

At matched rhyme performance, the on-policy model will remain more similar to the original student on non-poetry inputs than models trained on static data.

This should appear as:

* lower output-distribution divergence from the base model;
* higher layer-wise representation similarity;
* smaller losses on general-capability evaluations;
* and more localized parameter updates.

The proposed explanation is that on-policy gradients are applied to states already reachable by the student.

### H2: Static off-policy training is more likely to reorganize the planning computation

Both fixed-corpus SFT and teacher-trace SFT may encourage the model to build a new representation of the rhyme constraint at a structural boundary such as the newline.

On-policy distillation may instead preserve the student’s existing strategy of accessing the original rhyme word throughout generation.

The strongest supporting result would be:

* matched rhyme accuracy across conditions;
* a rhyme-word-to-newline causal handoff after static SFT;
* continued causal reliance on the rhyme word after OPD;
* and greater unrelated-task forgetting in the models that undergo the handoff.

### H3: Teacher-trace imitation may differ from generic static SFT

Teacher-trace SFT does more than expose the student to static prefixes. It also exposes the student to the stronger teacher’s preferred:

* rhyme words;
* line structures;
* vocabulary;
* semantic plans;
* and stylistic templates.

If teacher-trace SFT creates a newline handoff but fixed-corpus SFT does not, the change may reflect imitation of the teacher’s trajectory distribution rather than static teacher forcing alone.

If both conditions behave similarly, the relevant variable is more likely the use of fixed off-policy prefixes.

### H4: Prefix source matters independently of soft labels

Off-policy soft KD and on-policy soft KD will use the same divergence and teacher. Their primary difference will be whether the prefixes come from the teacher or the student.

If they differ mechanistically, that provides stronger evidence that the state distribution itself matters.

### H5: Reverse-KL OPD may reduce diversity

Poetry contains many valid next-token choices. A mode-seeking objective may achieve high rhyme accuracy by concentrating probability on a small number of common endings.

Diversity will therefore be treated as a primary evaluation axis rather than a secondary aesthetic concern.

## 4. Why Rhyming-Couplet Generation?

Rhyming couplets require a model to satisfy a future phonological constraint determined before the second line begins.

For example:

> She felt a sudden sense of fright,
> [generate the second line]

The generated line should end in a word such as *night*, *light*, or *sight*.

The model could implement this behavior in several ways:

1. repeatedly attend back to *fright*;
2. copy a phonological representation into the newline;
3. select the final rhyme word before generating the line;
4. represent an abstract rhyme family;
5. or delay rhyme selection until late in generation.

Prior work distinguishes between:

* **planning-compatible representations**, from which the future rhyme can be decoded;
* and **causally active planning sites**, where changing the activation redirects the generated rhyme.

The paper found that many models linearly encode future-rhyme information at the newline. However, only Gemma-3-27B causally relies on the newline representation. In that model, last-word patching becomes ineffective around layer 30 while newline patching becomes effective, indicating a causal handoff. 

This provides a measurable mechanistic phenotype that can be tracked during controlled training.

## 5. Scope

The main study will use:

* one model family;
* one student size;
* one teacher;
* one structured generation task;
* four training regimes;
* three random seeds per regime;
* and a fixed mechanistic-analysis suite.

The study will not attempt to show that every form of on-policy training behaves similarly. It will test whether the training-state distribution changes the mechanism learned in one controlled autoregressive task.

## 6. Models

### Student

The preferred student is **Gemma-3-4B**, using the same checkpoint variant and prompt formatting as the prior planning-site experiments.

This model is a useful starting point because it exhibits causal reliance on the original rhyme word but not a causal newline handoff. The model therefore begins with an identifiable computation that training can preserve, strengthen, or reorganize.

### Teacher

The preferred teacher is **Gemma-3-27B**.

This creates a particularly informative setup:

* the student initially relies on the rhyme word;
* the teacher exhibits a newline handoff;
* both use the same model family and tokenizer;
* and the experiment can test whether behavioral distillation transfers the teacher’s internal strategy.

### Initialization

Every trained condition begins from the same frozen student checkpoint.

The following will be matched wherever possible:

* prompt order;
* optimizer;
* learning-rate schedule;
* training-token budget;
* batch size;
* maximum response length;
* sampling temperature;
* and number of updates.

Full-parameter fine-tuning is preferred. Adapter-based training would constrain where mechanistic changes can occur and introduce an additional architectural confound.

## 7. Dataset Construction

## 7.1 Common task format

Every condition will use the same input-output structure:

```text
A rhyming couplet:
She felt a sudden sense of fright,
```

The target is a complete second line ending in a valid rhyme.

The first-line prompt distribution will be shared across conditions. What differs is how the target second lines and training prefixes are obtained.

## 7.2 Prompt pool

The study will construct approximately:

* 10,000 training prompts;
* 1,000 validation prompts;
* 1,000 in-distribution test prompts;
* 1,000 held-out-rhyme-family test prompts;
* and 500 recovery prompts.

The exact training size may be reduced following a pilot if the task saturates quickly.

## 7.3 Rhyme-family balancing

First-line rhyme words will be grouped by stressed vowel and subsequent phonemes using the same general phonological framing as the prior paper.

The prompt pool will be balanced so that common rhyme families do not dominate.

Evaluation will separately measure:

* unseen prompts from training rhyme families;
* entirely unseen rhyme families;
* common rhyme families;
* and rare rhyme families.

## 7.4 Fixed external poem corpus

The fixed-corpus SFT condition will use existing rhyming couplets or poem lines written independently of the experiment’s teacher.

Potential data sources may include public-domain poetry corpora or a curated existing couplet dataset. Every example must be transformed into the same format:

[
\text{first line} \rightarrow \text{second line}.
]

The corpus should be filtered so that:

* the final words form an identifiable rhyme;
* both lines are grammatically complete;
* pronunciation is unambiguous;
* formatting matches the other conditions;
* and the second line is not duplicated across many examples.

The fixed corpus must not consist of outputs generated by the teacher used in the other conditions.

## 7.5 Distribution matching

Fixed-corpus and teacher-generated datasets cannot be made perfectly identical, but they should be matched or reweighted on observable characteristics:

* number of examples;
* total training tokens;
* rhyme-family frequency;
* line length;
* final-word frequency;
* vocabulary difficulty;
* topic distribution;
* punctuation;
* and stylistic complexity.

Any remaining differences will be measured and reported.

## 7.6 Recovery prompts

Recovery prompts will include imperfect partial second lines:

> She felt a sudden sense of fright,
> but every door she tried was—

Other examples will contain repetition, semantic drift, or awkward syntax.

These evaluate whether a model can recover from states that are plausible under its own policy but unlikely in a curated teacher or poem corpus.

## 8. Experimental Conditions

The complete experiment contains one baseline and four trained conditions.

| Condition             | Prefix source            | Target                     | Main purpose                                |
| --------------------- | ------------------------ | -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Base                  | None                     | None                       | Measure the original behavior and mechanism |
| Fixed-corpus SFT      | Existing poem corpus     | Ground-truth poem tokens   | Measure generic static supervised learning  |
| Teacher-trace SFT     | Fixed teacher rollouts   | Sampled teacher tokens     | Measure hard-label off-policy distillation  |
| Teacher-trace soft KD | Fixed teacher rollouts   | Teacher token distribution | Control for hard versus soft supervision    |
| On-policy soft KD     | Current student rollouts | Teacher token distribution | Isolate student-visited training states     |

## 8.1 Fixed-corpus SFT

The model trains on existing poems:

[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{corpus}}
=============================

-\mathbb{E}*{(x,y)\sim D*{\mathrm{poems}}}
\sum_t \log \pi_\theta(y_t\mid x,y_{<t}).
]

These prefixes are static and off-policy with respect to the student, but they are not generated by the teacher.

This condition asks:

> Does ordinary static training on successful poems produce the same internal changes as imitating a stronger model?

## 8.2 Teacher-trace SFT

The teacher generates one or more fixed second lines for each prompt. The student then performs ordinary SFT on those traces:

[
y\sim\pi_T(\cdot\mid x)
]

[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{teacher\text{-}trace}}
===========================================

-\mathbb{E}*{x,y}
\sum_t
\log \pi*\theta(y_t\mid x,y_{<t}).
]

This is the main **off-policy distillation** condition.

It uses hard sampled teacher tokens rather than the full teacher distribution.

## 8.3 Teacher-trace soft KD

The prefixes are the same fixed teacher trajectories used in teacher-trace SFT, but the target at every position is the teacher’s token distribution:

[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{off\text{-}policy\ soft}}
==============================================

\mathbb{E}*{s\sim d*{\pi_T}}
D\left(
\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s),
\pi_T(\cdot\mid s)
\right).
]

This separates two possible explanations:

* the effect of student- versus teacher-generated prefixes;
* the effect of hard tokens versus dense teacher probabilities.

## 8.4 On-policy soft KD

The current student generates a response for each prompt. The teacher evaluates each student-generated prefix:

[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{on\text{-}policy}}
=======================================

\mathbb{E}*{s\sim d*{\pi_\theta}}
D\left(
\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s),
\pi_T(\cdot\mid s)
\right).
]

The primary divergence should be identical to that used for teacher-trace soft KD.

A reverse-KL objective is one candidate:

[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}
\left(
\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s)
\parallel
\pi_T(\cdot\mid s)
\right).
]

A mode-covering divergence may be added as a secondary condition only if the pilot reveals severe diversity collapse.

## 9. Experimental Comparisons

Each pairwise comparison answers a different question.

### Fixed-corpus SFT versus teacher-trace SFT

This isolates the effect of imitating teacher-generated trajectories relative to generic supervised training on static successful poems.

Possible interpretation:

* If both produce the same mechanism, static teacher forcing may be the relevant factor.
* If only teacher-trace SFT changes the mechanism, the teacher’s trajectory distribution may be responsible.

### Teacher-trace SFT versus teacher-trace soft KD

These conditions share the same prefixes.

The comparison isolates hard sampled labels versus dense teacher distributions.

### Teacher-trace soft KD versus on-policy soft KD

These conditions share the same teacher and token-level objective.

The comparison most cleanly isolates teacher-generated versus student-generated states.

### Fixed-corpus SFT versus on-policy KD

This is the practical comparison between ordinary supervised task training and on-policy teacher-guided learning.

## 10. Matching Training Conditions

Every condition will be evaluated in two ways.

## 10.1 Equal-compute comparison

Models will be compared after:

* the same number of student training tokens;
* the same number of optimizer updates;
* and approximately equal student-side computation.

Teacher inference cost will be reported separately.

For fixed-corpus SFT, examples may need to be repeated or subsampled to match the target-task token budget.

## 10.2 Matched-performance comparison

For every seed, select the earliest checkpoint that reaches a predetermined validation rhyme accuracy.

If one condition cannot reach the original threshold, use the highest threshold reached by every condition.

Mechanistic and retention claims will primarily rely on this matched-performance comparison.

## 10.3 Longitudinal checkpoints

Save checkpoints at approximately:

* initialization;
* 10%;
* 25%;
* 50%;
* 75%;
* and 100% of training.

This allows the study to observe when representations and causal pathways emerge.

## 11. Behavioral Evaluation

## 11.1 Target-task performance

Primary metrics:

* rhyme-family accuracy;
* valid-couplet rate;
* held-out-family rhyme accuracy;
* and recovery-prefix rhyme accuracy.

Secondary metrics:

* semantic coherence;
* grammaticality;
* relevance of the second line to the first;
* meter or approximate rhythm;
* and exact final-word prediction where appropriate.

## 11.2 Recovery performance

Models will be forced to continue from awkward or partially malformed prefixes.

This is where on-policy training should have its clearest behavioral advantage, because the prefixes resemble states the student may generate itself.

## 11.3 Diversity

Report:

* unique final rhyme words;
* entropy of the final-word distribution;
* distinct-1 and distinct-2;
* repeated-template frequency;
* pairwise semantic similarity;
* self-BLEU;
* and rhyme accuracy across multiple samples.

This guards against declaring a mode-collapsed OPD model superior merely because it repeatedly generates a few reliable rhymes.

## 11.4 Style-distribution analysis

Because fixed-corpus poems and teacher outputs may differ stylistically, evaluate:

* average line length;
* lexical diversity;
* syntactic patterns;
* topic frequency;
* teacher-likeness;
* and corpus-likeness.

This helps determine whether mechanistic changes correlate with the source style rather than the training policy.

## 12. Measuring Forgetting

## 12.1 Output-distribution drift

On a frozen non-poetry corpus, calculate divergence between the base and trained models:

[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}
\left(
\pi_{\mathrm{base}}(\cdot\mid s)
\parallel
\pi_{\mathrm{trained}}(\cdot\mid s)
\right).
]

Inputs will include:

* ordinary prose;
* factual questions;
* mathematical text;
* code;
* and general instructions.

Poetry-related examples will be excluded.

## 12.2 General capability retention

Use a compact evaluation suite covering:

* general language modeling;
* factual knowledge;
* instruction following;
* mathematical reasoning;
* and code completion.

Scores will be reported as changes relative to the shared base checkpoint.

## 12.3 Representation drift

Collect layer-wise residual-stream activations on the same frozen general-text corpus.

Compare each trained model with the base using:

* linear CKA;
* activation cosine similarity;
* representation norms;
* and orthogonal-Procrustes-aligned similarity.

The primary prediction is that OPD will produce more task-selective changes:

[
\text{large change on poetry}
]

but

[
\text{small change on general text}.
]

## 12.4 Parameter-update distribution

Report:

* layer-wise update norms;
* attention-versus-MLP update magnitude;
* embedding and unembedding changes;
* and concentration of update energy.

This is a descriptive complement to the activation analysis rather than direct evidence of a circuit.

## 13. Linear Probing

The probe methodology will follow the paper’s distinction between decodable information and causally used information. 

## 13.1 Probe targets

Train linear probes to predict:

1. the exact final rhyme token;
2. the final rhyme family;
3. a shuffled-label control;
4. and optionally the teacher’s intended final rhyme family.

## 13.2 Probe positions

Probe residual states at:

* the original rhyme word;
* punctuation;
* the newline;
* the first generated token;
* the second generated token;
* and the third generated token.

Sweep all layers.

## 13.3 Primary statistic

Define:

[
\Delta_{\mathrm{newline}}
=========================

\max_\ell
\left[
A_{\ell,\mathrm{newline}}
-------------------------

A_{\ell,\mathrm{first\ generated}}
\right].
]

This measures whether rhyme-relevant information is unusually concentrated at the newline.

## 13.4 Longitudinal analysis

Run probes at every saved checkpoint.

This can reveal whether:

* newline information appears before becoming causal;
* static SFT causes gradual migration;
* teacher-trace SFT transfers the teacher’s representation profile;
* or OPD strengthens the existing rhyme-word representation without relocating it.

## 13.5 Cross-condition probe transfer

Train a probe on one condition and evaluate it on another.

Examples:

* train on teacher activations, test on teacher-trace student activations;
* train on the base student, test after OPD;
* train on fixed-corpus SFT, test on teacher-trace SFT.

Higher cross-model transfer would suggest aligned representation geometry, while low transfer despite similar behavior would suggest different internal encodings.

## 14. Activation Patching

## 14.1 Clean and corrupt prompts

Construct matched pairs:

```text
Clean:
She felt a sudden sense of fright,

Corrupt:
She felt a sudden sense of fear,
```

Patch the corrupt activation into the clean run:

[
h_{\ell,i}^{\mathrm{clean}}
\leftarrow
h_{\ell,i}^{\mathrm{corrupt}}.
]

A patch is successful if the generated second line changes from the clean rhyme family to the corrupt rhyme family.

## 14.2 Positions

Patch:

* the rhyme word;
* punctuation;
* newline;
* and the first several generated positions.

## 14.3 Screening sweep

For each model and seed:

* use at least 20 clean-corrupt prompt pairs;
* draw approximately 10 stochastic completions per pair;
* and sweep every second layer at the rhyme-word and newline positions.

## 14.4 Dense sweep

For conditions exhibiting possible migration:

* sweep every layer in the transition region;
* use at least 50 prompt pairs;
* draw approximately 20 samples per pair;
* and test the full local position range.

## 14.5 Handoff definition

Let:

[
C_{\ell,i}
==========

P(\text{corrupt rhyme}\mid\text{patch at layer }\ell,\text{ position }i).
]

Define:

[
H_\ell
======

## C_{\ell,\mathrm{newline}}

C_{\ell,\mathrm{rhyme\ word}}.
]

A handoff requires:

1. negative (H_\ell) in earlier layers;
2. positive (H_\ell) in later layers;
3. uncertainty intervals excluding zero around the relevant regions;
4. and replication in at least two of three seeds.

A high newline probe score alone is not sufficient.

## 15. Head and Path Patching

This analysis will be run only when a trained model develops a causal newline site.

The procedure will:

1. rank attention heads by attention from the newline to the original rhyme word;
2. patch individual and top-(k) head outputs;
3. compare with random head sets;
4. compare with punctuation-attending control heads;
5. test MLP components;
6. and perform two-stage path patching.

The hypothesized path is:

[
\text{rhyme word}
\rightarrow
\text{attention heads}
\rightarrow
\text{newline}
\rightarrow
\text{future rhyme}.
]

This will determine whether the learned handoff is:

* sparse;
* diffuse;
* attention-mediated;
* MLP-mediated;
* or mechanistically similar to the teacher’s pathway.

The goal is not to require the student to reproduce the teacher’s exact head identities. Layer and head numbers are not directly comparable across model sizes. Instead, compare higher-level circuit properties:

* number of causally sufficient heads;
* relative layer depth;
* attention source and destination;
* and fraction of the residual-stream patching effect recovered.

## 16. Testing Mechanistic Inheritance

Teacher-trace SFT raises a specific question:

> Does the student imitate only the teacher’s outputs, or also converge toward the teacher’s internal strategy?

Several analyses will address this.

### Planning-site similarity

Compare whether teacher-trace SFT moves the student’s causal locus toward the teacher’s newline site.

### Layer-relative alignment

Express layers as normalized depth:

[
r_\ell=\frac{\ell}{L}.
]

Compare whether handoffs occur at similar relative depths.

### Probe-subspace alignment

Compare rhyme-probe directions across teacher and students after mapping activations into comparable low-dimensional spaces.

### Causal pathway structure

Compare whether both models use sparse attention-mediated routing from the rhyme word to the newline.

Evidence of similar outputs without similar pathways would indicate behavioral distillation without mechanistic inheritance.

## 17. Connecting Mechanism to Forgetting

For every condition and checkpoint, the study will produce:

1. target-task performance;
2. planning-site organization;
3. general behavior retention;
4. general representation drift;
5. and parameter-update localization.

The central analysis will ask whether greater forgetting is associated with:

* creation of a new newline site;
* weakening of the original rhyme-word pathway;
* larger changes in general-text representations;
* greater diffusion of updates across layers;
* or greater similarity to the teacher’s internal organization.

A particularly strong result would be:

[
\text{Fixed-corpus SFT, teacher-trace SFT, and OPD reach matched rhyme accuracy},
]

but:

* teacher-trace SFT develops the teacher-like newline handoff;
* fixed-corpus SFT develops either a different handoff or a diffuse new mechanism;
* OPD preserves the base student’s rhyme-word pathway;
* and OPD causes the least general representational drift.

That would demonstrate that the source of training trajectories affects not only what behavior is learned, but how the behavior is implemented.

## 18. Statistical Analysis

Each trained condition will use three seeds.

Behavioral confidence intervals will be computed over prompts.

Patching results will use hierarchical bootstrap resampling over:

1. training seeds;
2. prompt pairs;
3. stochastic generations.

Primary comparisons:

1. fixed-corpus SFT versus teacher-trace SFT;
2. teacher-trace SFT versus teacher-trace soft KD;
3. teacher-trace soft KD versus on-policy soft KD;
4. fixed-corpus SFT versus on-policy soft KD;
5. each trained condition versus the base student.

Primary endpoints:

* matched-performance retention score;
* general-text output KL;
* representation drift;
* peak rhyme-word patching rate;
* peak newline patching rate;
* handoff strength;
* held-out-family rhyme accuracy;
* and output diversity.

Claims about layer localization will use predefined regions and handoff criteria rather than selecting isolated peaks after viewing the results.

## 19. Possible Outcomes

### Both static SFT conditions migrate, while OPD does not

This would support the hypothesis that static off-policy teacher forcing encourages planning-site reorganization.

### Only teacher-trace SFT migrates

The mechanism may be transferred from, or induced by, the teacher’s particular response distribution.

This would distinguish teacher imitation from generic static SFT.

### Fixed-corpus SFT and OPD preserve the base pathway, but teacher-trace SFT migrates

This would be especially strong evidence of teacher-specific mechanistic inheritance.

### Teacher-trace SFT and teacher-trace soft KD differ

Hard versus soft supervision affects the learned mechanism even when the prefixes are identical.

### Teacher-trace soft KD and OPD differ

The source of the training states matters independently of the teacher and divergence.

### All conditions produce the same mechanism

Planning-site formation may be determined primarily by the task, architecture, and scale rather than the training regime.

### All conditions learn different mechanisms

The behavioral task may admit several computational solutions, with training data and objectives selecting among them.

### OPD achieves high rhyme accuracy but low diversity

The apparent gain is partly mode collapse. An entropy-aware objective would become the natural follow-up.

### No trained 4B model develops a newline handoff

The original Gemma-3-27B phenomenon may require greater capacity or broader training. The study can still compare causal strength, layer localization, representation drift, robustness, and circuit concentration.

## 20. Risks and Mitigations

### The fixed poem corpus differs too much from teacher data

Match or reweight observable properties and report remaining distributional differences. Include a subset analysis using prompts for which both corpus and teacher targets are available.

### The base model already performs the task well

Use rarer rhyme families, longer lines, stricter semantic requirements, or a smaller student.

### Training produces little measurable forgetting

Increase task-training intensity while retaining matched-performance comparisons. Output KL and representation drift may detect interference before benchmark scores decline.

### OPD is unstable

Use gradient clipping, shorter rollouts, controlled sampling, and a shared warm start applied to every branch.

### Fixed-corpus examples are noisy

Filter pronunciations, remove weak or slant rhymes, and manually audit a held-out sample.

### Mechanistic differences are caused by output-style differences

Evaluate style and diversity, and repeat patching on prompt subsets where all conditions produce outputs with matched length and rhyme-family frequency.

### Tokenization varies across examples

Pre-filter examples and record token indices explicitly for every prompt.

## 22. Success Criteria

The project will be successful if it establishes at least one of the following:

1. **Training-policy-dependent mechanisms**
   On-policy and static training reach similar rhyme accuracy but use different causal pathways.

2. **Teacher-specific mechanistic inheritance**
   Teacher-trace SFT causes the student to move toward the teacher’s planning-site organization, while fixed-corpus SFT does not.

3. **Generic static-SFT reorganization**
   Both fixed-corpus and teacher-trace SFT reorganize the student’s computation relative to OPD.

4. **Mechanistic reuse and retention**
   Preserving the original causal pathway is associated with lower forgetting and lower representation drift.

5. **A clear null result**
   After controlling for performance, compute, soft labels, dataset composition, and diversity, training-state source does not substantially alter the planning mechanism.

## Expected Contribution

The project will distinguish four ideas that are often conflated:

* learning from a fixed dataset;
* imitating fixed teacher trajectories;
* receiving dense teacher probabilities;
* and receiving teacher supervision on student-generated states.

Its central contribution will be a controlled test of whether these regimes produce the same observable capability through different internal computations.

Rather than asking only whether on-policy distillation improves performance or reduces forgetting, the project asks:

> Does on-policy training preserve and refine the student’s existing algorithm, while static supervised training replaces or relocates it?

The inclusion of both fixed-corpus SFT and teacher-trace SFT is essential. It allows the study to determine whether planning-site migration is a general consequence of static supervised learning, a specific consequence of imitating a stronger teacher, or a phenomenon unrelated to either.
